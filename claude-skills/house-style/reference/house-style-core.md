# House Style Core

The durable writing standard, plus rewrite patterns and the safety boundary.
This file is self-contained so the skill works with no repo present.

## Core Standard

Write for the reader's purpose, context, and time. Every sentence should help
the reader understand the point, make a decision, take action, or trust the
work.

## Default Rules

- State the main point early.
- Use plain language.
- Prefer short sentences and direct verbs.
- Keep paragraphs short, one main idea each.
- Use concrete nouns, numbers, dates, owners, and examples when available.
- Define acronyms on first use.
- Separate facts, assumptions, inferences, recommendations, and open questions
  when it matters.
- Avoid filler phrases and weak modifiers.
- Avoid jargon unless the reader uses it.
- Avoid repeated contrast scaffolding such as `not X, but Y` or paired
  `can / cannot` lines.
- Do not use Unicode em dash characters.

## Preferred Replacements

- `use` instead of `utilize`
- `help` or `improve` instead of vague `drive`
- `clear` or `specific` instead of `robust`
- direct standards instead of negative framing
- one direct statement of ownership instead of paired `can / cannot` lines

## Rewrite Patterns

### Contrast scaffolding

Before: `A checklist can remind me to separate facts from assumptions. It cannot
do the thinking for me.`

After: `A checklist owns repeatable prompts. The writer owns the thinking.`

### Generic closing

Before: `This framework paves the way for a brighter future of writing.`

After: `This framework gives reviewers a place to start.`

### Weak evidence

Before: `AI writing is usually worse because it sounds generic.`

After: `AI-assisted drafts often need review for generic tone, weak evidence, and
missing author judgment.`

### Filler

Before: `It is important to note that the system should be used thoughtfully.`

After: `Use the system thoughtfully.`

## What Automation Checks

Automation flags repeatable surface risks. These include em dashes and filler
phrases. They also include weak modifiers, jargon, stock conclusions, and
overloaded sentences. Automation also flags acronym candidates, repeated
contrast scaffolds, and known AI voice patterns.

## What Human Review Owns

Human review owns factual accuracy, evidence quality, source fidelity, and
missing context. It also owns recommendation strength, reader fit, and final
accountability.

## Exception Policy

A flagged phrase can stand when it is required by a quote or source title. It
can also stand for a product name, technical term, or deliberate voice choice.
Keep the reason short. Do not rewrite source material only to satisfy a style
rule.

## Authorship Boundary

This system improves writing quality. It does not detect authorship.

Do not use it to accuse anyone of using AI, to score human authorship, to
disguise AI use, or to bypass disclosure rules. AI-like patterns appear in human
writing, and human writing can be edited until it looks generic. Style signals
are weak evidence for authorship and strong prompts for review. Treat every
flag as a review prompt: does this wording help the reader trust and use the
work?
