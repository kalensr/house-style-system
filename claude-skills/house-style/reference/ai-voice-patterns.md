# AI Voice Patterns

Use this when writing or reviewing executive, recruiter-facing, public, and
leadership prose. The goal is to remove the default AI business voice, not only
to match the author's voice.

AI voice is a pattern, not one forbidden word list:

- abstract labels before concrete facts,
- polished but impersonal business language,
- over-neat sentence scaffolds,
- broad diagnosis before proof,
- role-fit language that sounds generated,
- nouns stacked in place of visible action,
- claims that compress several real events into one vague phrase.

## Repair Recipe

When a sentence sounds AI-generated, name the real thing:

1. actor,
2. action,
3. system or workflow,
4. before state,
5. friction or risk,
6. decision or ownership,
7. result or after state.

## Center Of Gravity Check

AI voice often pushes the wrong noun into subject position. When the real topic
is people adapting to AI, keep people in subject position. When the topic is
teams changing work or organizations making decisions, keep that actor visible.
Do not make AI, agents, abstract work, or nominalized actions the protagonist.

Check three levels:

- Sentence: who is the subject, and should they be?
- Paragraph: what entity controls the paragraph?
- Topic: what is the piece actually about?

Watch for:

- `AI is...`
- `agents are...`
- `the work is...`
- `the pattern...`
- `adoption requires...`

Repair by naming the person, team, customer, or organization. You can also name
the decision, workflow, or concrete change.

## Watchword List

Allow these abstract nouns only when the sentence also names the concrete
system, symptom, owner, or before-and-after change:

`ownership`, `visibility`, `alignment`, `strain`

`friction`, `complexity`, `modernization`

Also watch: `the roles that fit`, `the work`, `the pattern`, and the template
`where X needs Y`.

## The Seven Stop Patterns

### 1. Generic role-fit framing

Weak: `The opportunities that match my background`

State the role family directly. Do not narrate the matching logic.

Better: `I am interested in senior technology leadership roles, including...`

### 2. Abstract business-need clauses

Weak: `where the reporting platform needs stronger ownership`

If using `ownership`, name what is owned.

Better: `where core applications, delivery, support, and vendor decisions need
clearer executive ownership`

### 3. Pattern-recognition openings

Weak: `The pattern across these projects is familiar`

Show the situation first. Let the pattern emerge from the facts.

Better: `I joined a mid-sized company during a period of significant
operational and technology challenges.`

### 4. Flattened before-state labels

Weak: `The environment was fragmented, reactive, and dependent on manual handoffs`

Use specific facts for the before state. Use one broad summary only if the
surrounding detail earns it.

Better: `software development was outsourced, several systems needed
modernization, and the organization was working through urgent platform risk`

### 5. Vague consequence phrases

Weak: `introduced workflow friction`

If using `strain` or `friction`, name the visible symptom. Apply the same rule
to `complexity` and `risk`.

Better: `created support escalations, process confusion, and extra work for the
teams using the system`

### 6. Coordinated abstraction

Weak: `Ownership and delivery move together`

Replace `visibility`, `alignment`, and `move together`. Say what someone sees,
decides, changes, or owns.

Better: `leaders can see delivery progress, platform risk, support issues, and
vendor tradeoffs early enough to act`

### 7. Empty work nouns

Weak: `The work is not just documenting requirements.`

Name the work. Do not call it `the work` unless the prior sentence already made
it concrete.

Better: `Modernization also requires decisions about ownership, release
practices, support, vendor accountability, and risk.`

## Replacement Checklist

Before accepting a draft, scan for these:

- a polished label instead of an observable fact,
- `ownership`, `visibility`, `alignment`, `strain`, `complexity`, `friction`, or
  `modernization` with no concrete symptom named,
- `the work`, `the pattern`, `the roles that fit`, or `where X needs Y`,
- prose that sounds like a recruiter or consultant describing the writer from
  the outside,
- the lesson explained before the situation is shown,
- a contrast formula such as `not only`, `not just`, or `not X but Y`.

If yes, revise toward concrete operating language.

## Preferred Recruiter-Facing Order

This order is a container. It holds form only. The writer supplies whatever role
and content is current. No specific role is fixed here.

```text
polite opening
-> clear role family
-> before state
-> what changed
-> proof across career stages
-> current search boundary
-> simple invitation
```

Do not over-teach the fit. Show the pressure, what changed, and why the
background is useful. Let the reader infer some of the positioning.

## Quality Bar

A draft is acceptable only when:

- the reader can see the situation before the conclusion,
- claims are supported by facts or framed as judgment,
- the before state and after state are visible when relevant,
- abstract nouns are replaced with concrete systems, decisions, or risks,
- the close sounds like a human invitation, not a positioning filter.
