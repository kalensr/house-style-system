# Domain Modes

The core house style rules apply everywhere. Each mode adds a few checks for the
kind of writing in front of you.

## plain

Make the text clear, direct, and usable. This is the default.

## executive

Use when a leader needs to decide, approve, redirect, or understand a tradeoff.

- State the recommendation or decision early.
- Keep evidence close to the claim it supports.
- Make tradeoffs and risks visible.
- Name owners, dates, and next steps.
- Remove throat-clearing.

## leadership-reflection

Use for leadership or strategy reflection, public essays, and speeches when the
author asks for that review.

- Open on a real situation, pressure, question, or opportunity.
- Let the reader see the tension before the conclusion.
- Preserve first-person inquiry where it belongs.
- Show the path from insight to action.
- Tie leadership to service, team, or mission when the piece calls for it.
- Do not turn personal reflection into generic executive prose.

## personal-positioning

Use for recruiter-facing writing, executive search, and cover letters. This mode
is a structural container. It fixes the order, not the role. The writer supplies
the role family and content each time.

- Open politely.
- State the role family directly. Do not narrate the matching logic.
- Show the before state and what changed.
- Use proof across career stages.
- State the current search boundary.
- Close with a plain invitation.
- Do not over-teach the fit. Apply the AI voice patterns closely here.

## business

Use when the writing supports an update, recommendation, or decision.

- State the decision, update, or recommendation early.
- Name the owner and next step when action is needed.
- Make risks and tradeoffs visible.
- Separate facts from judgment.

## blog

Use when the writing is public and point-of-view driven.

- Make one strong idea visible.
- Preserve the author's point of view.
- Use concrete examples.
- Support claims or frame them as experience.
- Avoid generic uplift and stock conclusions.

## long-form-report

Use when the work needs synthesis, evidence, and structure across sections.

- Give each section a clear purpose.
- Lead each section with its main claim.
- Connect evidence to implications.
- Define technical terms.
- End with a decision path, recommendation, or research conclusion.

## student-essay

Use for learning, revision, and tutoring contexts.

- Preserve the student's level and voice.
- State the claim.
- Connect cited evidence to the claim.
- Treat style signals as review prompts, never as proof of authorship.

## social-post

Use for LinkedIn, X, announcements, and short public posts.

- Make one clear point for a specific audience.
- Include one concrete detail.
- Avoid generic uplift and stock conclusions.

## informal

Use for team notes, quick feedback, or practical updates.

- Put the practical context first.
- Use a natural tone. Allow contractions and short fragments when they help.
- Keep warmth without adding filler.
